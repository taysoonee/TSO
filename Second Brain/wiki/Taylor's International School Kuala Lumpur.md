## Update (May 13, 2026): TISKL Cannibalisation Risk (Project Falcon)
- **M&A Insight:** During the March 2023 evaluation of St. John International School ([[Project Falcon]]), a significant cannibalisation risk was identified for **TISKL**. If Taylor's Schools acquired a budget segment school within a 10km radius, price-sensitive parents might shift from TISKL to the budget option, expecting Taylor's quality without the premium facilities. This highlights the delicate positioning required to prevent internal portfolio competition, especially given the ongoing pressures of [[T20 Inflation Squeeze]].

## Update (May 13, 2026): Enrolment Inflow from GIS Squeeze
An analysis of September 2021 leaver data from [[Garden International School]] (GIS) indicates that TIS KL is the primary beneficiary of families leaving the premium tier due to "Financial Reason". This positions TIS as a critical defensive asset within the portfolio, capturing domestic wealth segments impacted by inflation, directly validating [[The Living Ledger]]'s [[T20 Inflation Squeeze]].

## Update (May 13, 2026): TISKL Historical Enrolment & Leaver Database Insight
- **Historical Database (1997-2024):** Extraction of 11,145 historical student records for TISKL (including its legacy Sri Garden phase) confirms long-term attrition trends. 
- **Expatriate & Domestic Squeeze:** The data validates both primary theses from [[The Living Ledger]]. 946 historical leavers cited "Relocation" or "Return to home country," heavily driven by expatriates from India (1,055 total historical records), China (664), South Korea (566), and Japan (235) (validating [[Thesis 1: The Expatriate & FDI Inflow Vector]]). Conversely, 203 direct financial withdrawals ("Financial Hardship" and "Termination-Fees") highlight the ongoing pricing vulnerability of the domestic core (6,072 Malaysian records) (validating [[T20 Inflation Squeeze]]).
- **Upward & Lateral Leakage:** Destination tracking shows TISKL losing students upwards to Tier-1 premiums ([[Garden International School]] [20], [[Alice Smith International School]] [19], [[Project ISKL Campus Bid|ISKL]] [18]) but also laterally to mid-tier/faith-based schools (St. John [18], Stella Maris [10], Wesley Methodist [9]).

## Update (May 13, 2026): TISKL Jan 2023 Intake & Funnel Concentration

## Intelligence Update (May 14, 2026): TISKL Current Enrolment & Post-COVID Recovery
- **Database Analysis:** An audit of the TISKL master database (11,896 total records) reveals a strong post-pandemic recovery with peak recent intakes in 2022 (235 new entries) and 2023 (179 new entries). The campus currently maintains an active pipeline of 1,221 Current Pupils, supported by a massive top-of-funnel backlog of 5,954 'Prospective' leads.
- **Demographic Split:** The active student body remains highly localized at ~58% (707 Malaysian students), while the 42% international segment is driven by Expatriates from India (182), China (84), and South Korea (38).
- **The Competitor Defection Barbell Effect:** Destination data for recent leavers (2020-2023) highlights a barbell defection trend. Wealthier segments are upgrading to Tier-1 schools ([[Garden International School]] 18, Alice Smith 16, ISKL 16), while price-sensitive families are downgrading to budget-tier and mission schools (St. John 15, Stella Maris 9, Wesley Methodist 8, Sri Sempurna 7). This explicitly confirms the squeezing effect outlined in [[The Living Ledger]].
- **Attrition Dynamics:** Expatriate flight remains high, with "Relocation" (403) and "Return to home country" (334) citing the primary reasons among recent leavers, validating [[Thesis 1: The Expatriate & FDI Inflow Vector]]. Concurrently, domestic financial pressures caused 172 leavers to cite "Financial Hardship" or "Termination-Fees", reinforcing the severe elasticity linked to [[T20 Inflation Squeeze]].

## Update (May 14, 2026): TISKL Enrolment Snapshot & Funnel Metrics (Sept 2021 Database)
- **Historical Top-of-Funnel Leakage:** An analysis of the September 2021 TISKL database (10,654 records) reveals a multi-year contraction. Peak new entries occurred in 2016 (429) but steadily deteriorated to 153 by 2021. Over the same period, withdrawals (leavers) accelerated, peaking at 394 in 2020. This indicates severe negative net enrolment during the pandemic window, highlighting the necessity for [[Admissions & Operations]] to rebuild the acquisition funnel.
- **Attrition Drivers:** Beyond graduation, the leading causes for withdrawal were "Relocation" (446), "Return to Home Country" (366), and "Financial Hardship" (130). This definitively validates the twin pressures of expatriate flight ([[Thesis 1: The Expatriate & FDI Inflow Vector]]) and domestic pricing sensitivity ([[T20 Inflation Squeeze]]).
- **Feeder School Strategy:** The primary sources of new student intakes are Kindergartens (860), Government Chinese Schools (732), and other Private/International schools (656). For future campaigns, Marketing must heavily target Chinese vernacular primary school parents seeking an international secondary transition, positioning TISKL as the premium bridge.
- **Demographic Resilience:** While the TISKL student body is predominantly domestic (Malaysian: 5,898), it maintains a vital secondary core of Asian expatriates, driven by India (981), China (568), and South Korea (552). Pastoral care and onboarding must remain culturally optimized to retain these specific expatriate segments.
